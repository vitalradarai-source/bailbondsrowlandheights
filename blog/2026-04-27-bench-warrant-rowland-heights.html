What a Bench Warrant Actually Is

A bench warrant is an arrest order issued by a judge — literally from the bench in court — for non-compliance with a court order. It is different from an arrest warrant for a new crime. A bench warrant says: "the court ordered you to do something, you didn't do it, and now law enforcement is authorized to bring you in."

The most common reasons a Los Angeles County judge issues a bench warrant are:

  • Failure to Appear (FTA): You missed a scheduled court date — arraignment, pretrial, sentencing, or even a traffic ticket hearing
  • Failure to Pay: You didn't pay a court-ordered fine, restitution, or fee on time
  • Probation Violation: You missed a probation check-in, failed a drug test, or didn't complete a court-ordered class
  • Failure to Comply with a Court Order: Missed community service, restitution payments, or required programs

Once issued, the warrant is entered into the LA County Sheriff's database and the California Law Enforcement Telecommunications System (CLETS) within 24 to 48 hours. From that moment, any encounter with any California law enforcement agency — even a simple traffic stop in another county — can result in arrest.

Critical Fact

Bench warrants in California do not expire. There is no statute of limitations on a bench warrant. A 10-year-old missed traffic court date can still cause an arrest at a sobriety checkpoint tonight.

How Bench Warrants Happen in Los Angeles County

Most people who get a bench warrant didn't deliberately ignore the court. The most common stories we hear from Rowland Heights families involve simple, fixable mistakes:

  • The court mailed the hearing notice to an old address
  • The defendant moved between San Gabriel Valley cities and didn't update their address with the court clerk
  • A traffic ticket was paid but the proof wasn't filed correctly
  • A probation officer was changed and the new schedule wasn't communicated
  • Family emergency or hospitalization made attendance impossible — but no continuance was filed
  • Confusion between the Pomona Courthouse and the Clara Shortridge Foltz Criminal Justice Center for downtown LA cases

None of those reasons matter to the warrant database. Once issued, the warrant is active — regardless of how reasonable the underlying explanation is. The good news: judges generally do consider those explanations at a recall hearing, especially when the person voluntarily addresses the warrant before being arrested.

How to Check If You Have a Warrant in Rowland Heights

If you have any reason to suspect a warrant might be active — a missed court date, an unresolved ticket, lapsed probation contact — check before law enforcement does. Three ways:

  1. Call the Los Angeles Superior Court Clerk: Pomona Courthouse handles most Rowland Heights cases at (909) 620-3120. Provide your full name and date of birth and ask if a warrant is active. The clerk can confirm but generally won't share details over the phone.
  2. LA Court Online Services: Visit www.lacourt.org, navigate to "Online Services" → "Case Search," and enter your case number if you have it. Note that this only works if you already know the case number.
  3. Hire a criminal defense attorney: Attorneys can perform a confidential warrant check via court records access without exposing you to arrest. This is the safest option if you're unsure or if there's an active criminal case.
Don't Walk Into the Courthouse to "Just Ask"

Some families think it's safer to drive to the courthouse and ask at the clerk window in person. This is risky. If a warrant is active, courthouse security or sheriff's deputies posted at the courthouse can arrest you on the spot — and you'll be processed without your bondsman or attorney ready. Always call first or go through an attorney.

What to Do When You Discover a Warrant

The moment you confirm a bench warrant exists, you have a window of opportunity to control the timing and outcome. Use it. The sequence we recommend to Rowland Heights families:

  1. Don't panic, don't run. Failing to clear a warrant only multiplies the consequences. Avoidance leads to worse charges (failure to appear can be filed as a separate misdemeanor or felony) and harsher bail amounts when you eventually are arrested.
  2. Call a criminal defense attorney first if possible. An attorney can sometimes file a motion to recall (quash) the warrant without you appearing in custody — especially for old failure-to-pay or failure-to-appear warrants on minor charges.
  3. Call Angels Bail Bonds at (626) 478-1062. Even before you walk into a courthouse or jail, we can pre-qualify you for a bail bond, prepare paperwork, and have everything ready so the moment a bond is required, it can be posted within minutes.
  4. Schedule a voluntary surrender or recall hearing. Coordinate with your attorney to either (a) surrender at the LASD Walnut/Diamond Bar Station with the bond ready or (b) attend a recall hearing where the judge may quash the warrant entirely.
  5. Show up clean and ready. Conservative clothes, no alcohol or substances, all relevant documents (proof of address, proof of compliance with prior court orders if available, employment letter if helpful). Judges respond to defendants who take the appearance seriously.

Walking In Voluntarily — The Smartest Move

Voluntary surrender — sometimes called a "walk-in" — almost always produces better outcomes than waiting to be arrested. Why:

  • Custody time is shorter. Walk-ins often spend hours in custody instead of days. The bond is pre-approved, paperwork is ready, and the court has scheduled time for your appearance.
  • Judges view it favorably. A defendant who voluntarily appears demonstrates accountability. Many judges will reduce bail amounts, quash the warrant entirely, or grant own-recognizance release on minor warrants when the appearance is voluntary.
  • You avoid the worst-case scenario. Being arrested at home, at work, or during a traffic stop in front of family is traumatic. It also means you're processed without your attorney, your bondsman, or your support system ready.
  • Background-check fallout is contained. An arrest record from a planned surrender looks very different on a background check than an arrest from a routine traffic stop with a "fugitive from justice" notation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bench warrant?

A bench warrant is an arrest order issued by a judge from the bench (in court) when someone fails to appear at a scheduled hearing, doesn't pay a court-ordered fine, or violates a condition of probation. Unlike an arrest warrant for a new crime, a bench warrant comes from the court itself for non-compliance.

How do I find out if I have a bench warrant?

Call the Pomona Courthouse clerk at (909) 620-3120, search the LA Court Online Services portal at lacourt.org, or hire a criminal defense attorney to perform a confidential check. Don't drive to the courthouse to ask in person — courthouse security can arrest you on the spot.

Can I be arrested at home for a bench warrant?

Yes. Bench warrants do not expire. The LA County Sheriff's Walnut/Diamond Bar Station can serve a warrant at home, work, or during any traffic stop. Background checks for employment, professional licensing, and gun purchases also trigger warrant detection.

What happens if I voluntarily turn myself in?

Voluntary surrender produces faster processing, shorter custody, and judges who view your appearance favorably. Coordinate with a bail bondsman first so the bond is ready — total custody time can drop from days to hours.

Can I post bail on a bench warrant before going to court?

Yes. Once a warrant has a bail amount, Angels Bail Bonds can post the surety bond at the LASD Walnut/Diamond Bar Station. Call (626) 478-1062 before walking in.

Will a bench warrant show on a background check?

Yes. Many California employer and licensing background checks detect open warrants. Clearing the warrant voluntarily — even posting bail and attending one hearing — is far better than being silently disqualified.
